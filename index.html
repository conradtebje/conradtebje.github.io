Major Project · Portsmouth · 2026

The Berm / Meridian Building

A civic maritime hub on Portsmouth's southern waterfront, designed to give back more than it takes. Three marine environmental charities (Blue Marine Foundation, the Marine Conservation Society, and Hampshire & Isle of Wight Wildlife Trust) occupy public galleries set alongside a shared research workshop, wet lab, and internal dock, so visitors can observe active research directly. A restaurant, cafe, lecture theatre, and residential apartments complete a building that reads as landscape before it reads as architecture.

About

Buildings that belong to where they are

I'm a Part I architecture graduate from the University of Portsmouth, currently seeking my first position in practice. Architecture, to me, is the discipline that takes what must exist and asks what it could become. I want to make places people choose to go to - spaces that don't require money to be enjoyed.

The Berm/Meridian Building, my major project, is a civic maritime hub on The Hard, Portsmouth's southern waterfront. A working marine research workshop and internal dock sit alongside public galleries curated by three marine environmental charities, so the public can observe active research directly. The building is designed to earn its place on a historically sensitive waterfront by giving back more than it takes.

I believe architecture is a long argument made in material, one that continues long after the architect has left. I try to make buildings that are honest about that: materials drawn from their context, programmes that serve more than their clients.

My dissertation examined public perception of Brutalism through Portsmouth's demolished Tricorn Centre, arguing that its failure was not architectural but the product of sustained neglect and hostile media framing. It reflects something I think about more broadly: how narrative shapes the reception of a place, and why demolition is so often a response to neglect rather than to design.

Dissertation · January 2026

Reframing Brutalism

The Tricorn Centre and Public Perception

Portsmouth's Tricorn Centre opened in 1966 as one of Britain's most ambitious Brutalist buildings. By 2001 it was voted the ugliest building in the country. It was demolished in 2004.

This dissertation investigates why. It argues that the Tricorn's failure was not inherent to its Brutalist architecture, but the result of poor siting, long-term neglect, and the cumulative effect of hostile media framing. Drawing on archival newspaper research at Portsmouth History Centre, the study reconstructs how public hatred was gradually constructed over decades, until the building became a scapegoat for anxieties that had nothing to do with concrete.

The research is accompanied by a triptych of abstract paintings tracing the building's emotional trajectory: from the optimism of its inception, through public rejection, to the unresolved absence left after demolition.
