Overview

A new-build nursery in Barnsbury Park, Portsmouth, designed around the needs of children from birth through to preschool age. The building is organised across two floors with a central outdoor play area at ground level and a roof terrace above, creating layered spaces for learning, play, and rest.

The scheme is informed by Montessori and Waldorf Steiner educational philosophies - both of which emphasise child-led exploration, natural materials, and environments that scale to the child rather than the adult. The building responds to this directly: portholes are set at children's eye level, nooks are sized for small bodies, and the raised platform gives children a sense of ownership over the space.

Materials are natural and warm throughout - timber, brick, and plaster in pastel shades that complement rather than compete with the activity inside. Colours are drawn from abstract shapes, giving the interior a sense of play without being prescriptive.

The plan is deliberately open and connected, allowing staff to maintain sight lines across the building while giving each age group - babies, toddlers, preschoolers - their own defined territory within the whole.

Brief and Concept

Designing for Children

The brief asked for a nursery that could serve children across all early years age groups within a Portsmouth site. Three potential locations within Barnsbury Park were assessed - I selected Site 3, the quietest and most sheltered of the three, despite its proximity to a pumping station to the east.

The central design question was how to make a building that genuinely belongs to its users. Standard institutional nurseries are designed at adult scale, with child-sized features added as afterthoughts. Here the intent was the reverse: scale the building to the child first, then accommodate the adults within it.

Precedent research focused on three kindergarten designs that each resolved this differently - Tezuka Architects' Fuji Kindergarten uses a single looping rooftop track to give children freedom of movement; HIBINOSEKKEI's ATG Kindergarten introduces soft transitions between interior and exterior; and TIANHUA's Shenzhen Nanshan Kindergarten uses colour and form to create a legible landscape of play. Each informed a different aspect of the final design.

Design Progression

From Concept to Final Form

Stage 01 - Concept and Plan

Early design work explored how to organise the three age groups - babies, toddlers, preschoolers - in relation to the site boundary and the outdoor play areas. The ground floor plan was developed iteratively, testing how corridor widths, nook positions, and the central play court worked together.

A key early decision was to wrap the building around the outdoor space rather than push it to the back. This keeps the garden central and visible from every major room.

Stage 02 - Elevations and Materials

With the plan settled, work moved to the elevations and material selection. The porthole windows were introduced as a response to the scale problem: standard windows sit above children's eye line. Portholes set at 600mm gave children an unobstructed view out without compromising the building's structural or thermal performance.

The pastel colour palette was applied to the elevation to reinforce the building's identity from the street, using abstract shapes rather than illustrative decoration.
